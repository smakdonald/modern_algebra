Proposition 1.83. The Automorphism Group.

The set of automorphisms of \(G\text{,}\) denoted \(\operatorname{Aut}(G)\text{,}\) is a group under composition.

Proof.

First, I show that operation is associative. Let \(\varphi, \phi, \psi\in\Aut(G)\text{.}\) As function composition is associative, we see \((\varphi\circ\phi)\circ\psi = \varphi\circ(\phi\circ\psi)\text{.}\)
I claim \(\id\) is the identity element of \(\Aut(G)\text{.}\) Recall from ExampleÂ 1.81 that \(\id:G\to G\) is indeed an automorphism. Let \(\varphi\in\Aut(G)\text{.}\) We will show \(\varphi\circ \id=\varphi=\id\circ \varphi\text{.}\) As all functions share the same domain and co-domain, we need only show that the functions map elements equally, Let \(x \in G\text{,}\) and observe:
\begin{align*} \varphi\circ \id(x) &= \varphi(\id(x)) \\ &= \varphi(x) \\ &= \id(\varphi(x)) \\ &= \id\circ\varphi(x) \end{align*}
Thus \(\id\) is indeed the identity element in \(\Aut(G)\text{.}\)
Finally, I show that every automorphism has an inverse, Let \(\varphi\in\Aut(G)\text{.}\) As \(\varphi\) is an isomorphism, there exists a homomorphism \(\phi: G \to G\) such that \(\varphi \circ \phi = \operatorname{id}\) and \(\phi \circ \varphi = \operatorname{id}\text{.}\) Thus \(\phi\) is, itself, an automorphism of \(G\text{,}\) and so \(\phi\in\Aut(G)\text{,}\) making \(\phi\) the inverse of \(\varphi\text{.}\)
