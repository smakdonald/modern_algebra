Theorem 1.78. Isomorphisms form “Equivalence” Relation.

  1. \(\displaystyle G\cong G\)
  2. If \(G\cong H\text{,}\) then \(H\cong G\)
  3. If \(G\cong H\) and \(H\cong K\text{,}\) then \(G\cong K\)

Proof.

  1. The identity map from \(G\to G\) is an isomorphism, making \(G\) isomorphic to \(G\text{.}\)
  2. Suppose \(\varphi:G\to H\) is an isomorphism. Then there exists a homomorphism \(\phi: H \to G\) such that \(\varphi \circ \phi = \operatorname{id}_H\) and \(\phi \circ \varphi = \operatorname{id}_G\text{.}\) Thus \(\phi\) is an isomorphism from \(H\) to \(G\text{,}\) and so \(H\cong G\text{.}\)
  3. Let \(\varphi: G\to H\) and \(\phi: H\to K\) be isomorphisms. From Theorem 1.65 we see \(\phi\circ\varphi\) is a homomorphism of groups. As both \(\varphi\) and \(\phi\) are injective, \(\phi\circ\varphi\) is injective by [cross-reference to target(s) "thm-compositions-and-jectivity" missing or not unique]. Similarly, as both \(\varphi\) and \(\phi\) are surjective, \(\phi\circ\varphi\) is surjective by [cross-reference to target(s) "thm-compositions-and-jectivity" missing or not unique]. Thus \(\phi\circ\varphi\) is a bijective homomorphism from \(G\) to \(K\text{,}\) we see \(G\cong K\) by Theorem 1.71
