Theorem 6.15. Semidirect Products are Groups.

If \(H\) and \(K\) are groups and \(\rho:K\to \operatorname{Aut}(H)\) is a homomorphism, then setting:
  1. \(H \rtimes_\rho K\) is a group
  2. \(H\cong H':=\{(h,e)\mid h\in H\}\mathrel{\unlhd}H \rtimes_\rho K\) and \(K\cong K':=\{(e,k)\mid k\in K\}\leq H \rtimes_\rho K\)
  3. \((H \rtimes_\rho K )/ H' \cong K\text{.}\)

Proof.

  1. The proof is straightforward but a bit messy. For associativity, note that
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} (y_1,x_1) \left( (y_2, x_2) (y_3, x_3) \right) &=(y_1,x_1) (y_2\rho(x_2)(y_3), x_2x_3)\\ &=(y_1\rho(x_1)\left(y_2\rho(x_2)(y_3)\right), x_1x_2x_3)\\ &=(y_1\rho(x_1)(y_2)(\rho(x_1)\circ \rho(x_2))(y_3), x_1x_2x_3)\\ &=(y_1\rho(x_1)(y_2)\rho(x_1x_2)(y_3), x_1x_2x_3)\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    On the other hand
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \left( (y_1,x_1) (y_2, x_2) \right) (y_3, x_3) & = (y_1 \rho(x_1)(y_2), x_1 x_2) (y_3, x_3) \\ & = (y_1 \rho(x_1)(y_2)\rho(x_1x_2)(y_3), x_1 x_2 x_3). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    This gives associativity.
    The fact that \((e,e)\) is a two-sided identity follows from the fact that \(\rho(e)(y) = \text{id}_H(y)=y\text{.}\)
    Finally
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} (y,x) (\rho(x^{-1})(y^{-1}) ,x^{-1}) &= (y \rho(x)\left(\rho(x^{-1})(y^{-1}) \right), e) = (y (\rho(x)\circ\rho(x^{-1}))(y^{-1}), e) \\ &= (y \rho(e)(y^{-1}), e) = (yy^{-1},e)=(e,e), \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    and similarly
    \begin{equation*} (\rho(x^{-1})(y^{-1}) ,x^{-1}) (y,x) = (e,e). \end{equation*}
  2. Define a funtion
    \begin{equation*} i: H \to H \rtimes_\rho K \end{equation*}
    as \(i(y) = (y, e)\text{.}\) Then \(i\) is a homomorphism, since
    \begin{equation*} i(y_1) i(y_2) = (y_1,e)(y_2,e) = (y_1\rho(e)(y_2) , ee) = (y_1 y_2, e) = i(y_1y_2). \end{equation*}
    The map is clearly injective and hence its image is isomorphic to \(H\text{.}\) In fact, the image is normal since the second component of
    \begin{equation*} (y,x) (y_2, e) (\rho(x^{-1})(y^{-1}), x^{-1}) \end{equation*}
    is clearly \(e\text{.}\) Let us write this image as
    \begin{equation*} H' := \{(y,e) \mid y \in H\} \unlhd H \rtimes_\rho K. \end{equation*}
    The function
    \begin{equation*} j: K \to H \rtimes_\rho K \end{equation*}
    defined by \(j(x) = (e,x)\) is also an injective homomorphism and thus its image
    \begin{equation*} K' := \{(e,x) \mid x \in H \} \leq H \rtimes_\rho K \end{equation*}
    is isomorphic to \(K\text{.}\) \(K'\) is typically not normal, however. Finally, it is easy to see that \(H'K'= H \rtimes_\rho K\) and \(H' \cap K' = \{e\}\text{.}\) Putting this all together we have
    • \(H' \unlhd H \rtimes_\rho K\text{,}\)
    • \(K' \leq H \rtimes_\rho K\text{,}\)
    • \(H' K' = H \rtimes_\rho K\text{,}\) and
    • \(H'\cap K' = \{e\}\text{.}\)
  3. Consider the projection onto the second factor \(\pi_2:H \rtimes_\rho K \to K\) given by \(\pi_2(y,x)=x\text{.}\) This is a goup homomorphism since the second component of \((y_1,x_1)(y_2,x_2)\) is \(x_1x_2\) and is surjective by definition. Now
    \begin{equation*} \operatorname{Ker}(\pi_2)=\{(y,e_K)\mid y\in H\}=H'\cong H. \end{equation*}
    By the First Isomorphism Theorem for Groups we conclude that \((H \rtimes_\rho K )/ H' \cong K\text{.}\)
